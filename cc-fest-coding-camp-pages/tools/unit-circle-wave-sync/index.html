Unit Circle Wave Sync

A point orbits the unit circle. As it moves, sin(θ) and cos(θ) trace waves to the right — live, in sync. The circle and the wave are the same thing seen two different ways.

Notice this: sin(θ) and cos(θ) are identical waves — but cos is exactly 90° ahead. That's because the x and y components of circular motion are the same oscillation, just out of phase. This is why cos(frameCount * 0.05) and sin(frameCount * 0.05 + HALF_PI) produce the same curve.
Break it on purpose: Set speed to ×2 and watch the wave build twice as fast. Now reset and watch at ×½. The wave shape is identical — only the time it takes to draw it changes. Speed changes the frequency of the wave in time, not its shape.
Remix this: In p5.js, try let y = sin(frameCount * 0.05) * 100 to make something oscillate vertically. Add a cos() for the x position and you get circular orbit: the point traces the unit circle from the inside.

Teacher move

Prompt Pause the animation at 90° (top of circle). Ask: what is sin(90°)? What is cos(90°)? Point at the wave and the circle projection lines — they should give the same answer.
Misconception Students expect sin and cos to be unrelated. They're the same wave offset by 90°. The dashed projection lines show this directly: sin is the vertical shadow, cos is the horizontal shadow of the same rotating point.
Ask After one full revolution: where does the wave end up? If you ran the animation for 10 full circles, what would the wave look like? (It would overwrite itself — it's periodic.)
