Data as Argument: When Numbers Tell Stories

W.E.B. Du Bois and Florence Nightingale showed that data visualization is not neutral — it is a choice about what to show, how to show it, and who it is for. The bar chart exercise is not just syntax. It is a statement.

W.E.B. Du Bois

1900 · Paris Exposition Universelle

Du Bois and his students created 63 hand-drawn data visualizations showing the social and economic progress of Black Americans — presented at the 1900 World's Fair in Paris to counter the narrative that Black Americans were incapable of advancement.

His charts used bold geometric forms, striking colors, and unconventional layouts — aesthetic choices that made the data impossible to ignore. The visualization was an argument.

"The problem of the twentieth century is the problem of the color line."

Florence Nightingale

1858 · Notes on Matters Affecting the Health of the British Army

Nightingale invented the polar area diagram (sometimes called a "coxcomb" chart) to show that most British soldiers in the Crimean War were dying from preventable diseases — not battle wounds.

She presented this to Parliament not as a statistic but as a visual argument so clear that policy changed: sanitation reform saved thousands of lives.

"To understand God's thoughts, we must study statistics, for these are the measure of his purpose."

Show as:

What you choose to show

Du Bois chose to show Black prosperity and achievement — data that existed but was rarely visualized. Nightingale chose to show preventable deaths, not total deaths. The selection of what to measure is already an argument.

How you choose to show it

A bar chart emphasizes comparison. A polar diagram emphasizes proportion and cycle. Dots emphasize individuality. Text removes visual weight entirely. Every form shapes what the viewer notices first.

Who you're showing it to

Du Bois addressed the world at a World's Fair. Nightingale addressed Parliament. You address your classroom, your community, your family. The audience shapes what the visualization needs to do.

Why the bar chart exercise matters

When you write names[i] and values[i] in your Session 4 sketch, you are not just learning syntax. You are learning how to take something you know — a number, a name, a measurement — and make it visible. Du Bois and Nightingale used that same power to change policy, shift perception, and argue for justice. The code is the beginning. What you say with it is the work.
