Every circle hides a triangle

The right triangle fits perfectly inside a unit circle. The angle θ at the center is the only thing that changes — the two legs always measure cos(θ) and sin(θ). Scale those legs by a radius, and you can reach any point on any circle. Let the angle grow over time, and circular motion emerges. Plot the y-value of that motion over time, and a sine wave appears. Triangles, circles, and waves are three views of the same relationship.

Teacher move

Prompt Go to Circle and change the speed value. What stays constant as the speed changes?
Misconception Students think sin() and cos() give a static ratio from a right triangle. Clarify: feeding them a number that grows over time turns them into oscillators — output that smoothly cycles between -1 and 1, which is exactly what circular motion needs.
Ask cos() controls x and sin() controls y. Why does the point trace a circle and not a straight line? What would happen if you used sin() for both?
